Technical White Paper

Passive Acoustic Monitoring for Salmon and Cod Farms

A continuous, low-cost communication layer for fjord-scale ecosystem monitoring
UnheardHeard • 2025

Executive Summary

For salmon and cod producers, the challenge is no longer lack of data inside the cage—it is knowing what is happening in the fjord around and between farms, 24/7, at a cost that actually scales.

Passive acoustic monitoring (PAM) is emerging as a realistic way to do exactly that: a low-cost, continuous "communication layer for nature" that listens to the soundscape and turns it into operational signals.

This white paper presents the scientific evidence for acoustic monitoring in aquaculture, including direct studies on salmon and cod, and outlines the practical value streams for farm operators. The technology is proven, costs are scalable, and global ocean science now recognizes sound as an essential variable for tracking ecosystem health.

1.Why Sound is a Proxy for Ecosystem Health

1.1 The Scientific Basis

Marine ecosystems are acoustically rich. Fish, invertebrates, mammals, and even habitats themselves produce characteristic sounds. The combined "soundscape" (biological sounds + physical noise) is closely linked to biodiversity, community structure, and environmental condition.

Published Evidence: Soundscapes Track Biodiversity

Royal Society Open Science (2020): The diversity of bioacoustic signals in ocean soundscapes can be used as a proxy for biodiversity, with richer communities producing more complex soundscapes.

Ecological Indicators (2024): Systematic review concludes that soundscape characteristics are consistently related to environmental variables and habitat condition. Acoustic indices are promising indicators of ecosystem health across many systems.

Coral reef and river studies: Biophonic (biological) sound levels and patterns track reef and riparian health. Degraded systems show simpler, quieter, or more intermittent soundscapes compared to healthy sites.

1.2 Ocean Sound as Essential Ocean Variable (EOV)

Global ocean observing programs have formally recognized "Ocean Sound" as an Essential Ocean Variable—a core variable that must be monitored to understand ocean health and biodiversity.

The 2023 Ocean Sound EOV implementation plan explicitly calls for:

  • Increased acoustic monitoring across marine environments
  • Development of low-cost underwater recorders for wide deployment
  • Integration of acoustic data into biodiversity assessments
  • Standardized methods for soundscape analysis
Key Finding: The soundscape is now treated as a measurable, scientifically backed indicator of ecosystem condition by the global ocean science community.

2.Direct Evidence for Salmon and Cod

2.1 Atlantic Salmon

There is direct, published evidence that sound carries useful, quantifiable information about salmon condition and behavior.

What This Means for Salmon Farmers

Salmon produce detectable acoustic signatures linked to feeding, stress, and normal behavior. These signals can be monitored continuously and analyzed in real-time to provide early warnings of welfare issues before they become visible through conventional observation.

2.2 Atlantic Cod

Atlantic cod are strongly sound-producing fish, particularly during spawning periods.

Key Finding: Both salmon and cod have identifiable acoustic signatures linked to behavior, reproduction, and stress. The science is emerging but the direction is clear: sound is becoming a practical welfare and behavior signal at farm scale.

3.From Cages to Fjords: Listening at Ecosystem Scale

3.1 Spatial Scale Advantages

Most high-impact acoustic work has focused on coral reefs, coastal habitats, and global ocean monitoring. However, the underlying principles apply directly to fjord systems around salmon and cod farms.

Key capabilities of fjord-scale PAM:

  • Survey habitats at multiple spatial scales—from individual cages to entire fjord basins
  • Deploy relatively inexpensive hydrophones that leverage natural sound propagation underwater
  • Differentiate fine-scale ecological states between sites and detect changes over time
  • Track shifts in species composition and human pressure impacts

3.2 Machine Learning Integration

A 2023/2024 wave of reviews on machine learning in ecoacoustics reports that AI now makes it feasible to automatically extract biodiversity, activity, and anomaly metrics from very large acoustic datasets.

What a Fjord-Scale PAM Network Can Track

Ecosystem vitality: How "alive" the soundscape is (biophony levels, complexity) at different points in the fjord over seasons and years

Anomaly detection: Sudden drops in biological sound outside multiple farms, or spikes in anthropogenic noise that might stress fish

Species monitoring: Spawning and migration activity of cod and other key species, plus presence of predators or marine mammals, over areas much larger than any single farm

3.3 Current Evidence Base

To be precise: we do not yet have a published paper that plots "fjord soundscape index" directly against "salmon or cod harvest per cage." What we do have is:

  • Strong cross-ecosystem evidence that soundscape metrics track biodiversity and ecosystem status
  • Direct evidence in salmon farming that stress and feeding state are acoustically detectable
  • Direct evidence that cod spawning and presence can be mapped acoustically over large marine areas
Scientific Assessment: Given that ecosystem health and biodiversity outside the nets affect farm risk and mortality, using sound as a continuous proxy outside the nets is scientifically grounded, even if exact ROI curves are still being quantified.

4.Value Streams for Farm Operators

From an operational perspective, passive acoustic monitoring over fjords and coastal zones offers four main value streams.

4.1 Early Warning = Fewer Losses

Evidence base:

  • Sound reveals stress states and abnormal behavior in salmon earlier than visual inspection (Aquacultural Engineering, 2024)
  • Fjord-scale soundscape changes can flag events threatening farms: harmful algal blooms, low-oxygen periods, unusual shipping noise, predator peaks

Economic impact: Even small percentage reductions in mortality (1-3%) have very large financial impact in high-value salmon and cod production. Given typical mortality rates of 15-17% in Norwegian salmon farming, reducing mortality by 2 percentage points represents approximately 12% improvement in survival.

4.2 Cheaper, Denser Environmental Monitoring

Table 3: Cost Comparison - Conventional vs. Acoustic Monitoring
Method Temporal Coverage Spatial Coverage Cost Profile
ROV surveys Episodic (monthly/quarterly) Localized transects High per-survey cost
Manual dives Episodic Very localized High labor cost, weather dependent
Grab samples Episodic Point samples Moderate cost, limited information
Passive Acoustic Monitoring Continuous (24/7) Fjord-scale coverage Low marginal cost after deployment

Regulatory alignment: PAM provides much higher temporal resolution at lower marginal cost, directly addressing regulatory requirements for documentation of environmental status and biodiversity at area scale, not just at cage level.

4.3 Evidence for Growth and Licensing

Norway's traffic-light system and similar frameworks link production volumes to environmental impact and wild stock health. Having continuous data on soundscape-based ecosystem indicators strengthens the case that a region is stable or improving.

Benefits for licensing applications:

  • Hard, continuous data supporting applications for increased biomass
  • Evidence base for new site approvals
  • Demonstration of environmental stewardship for traffic-light assessments

Because Ocean Sound is now an Essential Ocean Variable tied to biodiversity, data from a fjord-scale PAM network can feed directly into ESG reporting and future nature-related disclosure regimes.

4.4 Innovation Platform: AI and Nature-Based Risk Analytics

Modern ecoacoustics is advancing rapidly due to machine learning. Algorithms already classify species, detect anomalies, and compute biodiversity indices from raw audio.

A network of low-cost acoustic recorders effectively becomes a data platform for nature, enabling:

  • Welfare dashboards for salmon and cod
  • Biodiversity credits or "nature-positive" metrics if future markets reward verified ecosystem improvements
  • Risk models linking external ecosystem changes (soundscape shifts) to farm performance and insurance pricing

5.Implementation Framework

5.1 What is Solidly Established

When presenting PAM to decision-makers or boards, the following statements are backed by published science:

  1. Ocean soundscapes are recognized at the highest levels (GOOS, Ocean Sound EOV) as indicators of biodiversity and ecosystem health
  2. Salmon and cod produce characteristic sounds linked to behavior, spawning, and stress, detectable with PAM at commercially relevant scales
  3. PAM is a cost-effective, non-invasive method for long-term, large-area monitoring compared with traditional methods

5.2 What is Promising but Still Emerging

Areas where science is advancing but quantitative models are still developing:

  • Quantitative models that convert fjord-scale soundscape indices directly into predicted mortality reductions or yield gains for salmon and cod
  • Use of fjord-wide acoustic biodiversity metrics in formal licensing, traffic-light calculations, or biodiversity credit schemes

5.3 Recommended Positioning

Message to Operations Leadership

"We are adopting a scientifically backed, low-cost acoustic layer that lets the fjord talk to us continuously. The global science community now treats sound as a core variable for ocean health. In salmon and cod, we already see that behavior and stress are acoustically visible. We are turning that into earlier warnings, better welfare, and stronger documentation of nature-positive performance."

5.4 Practical Implementation Steps

Phase 1: Pilot Deployment (3-6 months)

  • Deploy 3-5 hydrophones in fjord system around existing farms
  • Establish baseline soundscape characterization
  • Correlate acoustic signals with known events (feeding times, weather, shipping)
  • Validate stress signature detection against visual observations

Phase 2: Operational Integration (6-12 months)

  • Expand to full fjord coverage (10-20 nodes depending on area)
  • Integrate acoustic data into daily operations dashboards
  • Develop alert thresholds for anomaly detection
  • Begin tracking seasonal patterns and long-term trends

Phase 3: Strategic Application (12+ months)

  • Use acoustic data in licensing applications and traffic-light documentation
  • Develop predictive models linking soundscape metrics to performance
  • Integrate with ESG reporting and sustainability certifications
  • Participate in industry-wide data sharing for regional ecosystem assessment

6.2 Potential Value Capture

For a typical Norwegian salmon farm producing 3,000-5,000 tons annually:

  • 2% mortality reduction at current prices (€7-8/kg) = €420,000-800,000 additional revenue
  • Early HAB warning preventing one major event = €500,000-2,000,000 loss avoided
  • Licensing support enabling 5% capacity increase = €1-2 million additional annual production value

Return on investment: If PAM prevents one significant loss event or enables small improvements in mortality, the system pays for itself within 1-2 years.

7.Conclusion

Passive acoustic monitoring represents a scientifically validated, cost-effective approach to continuous ecosystem monitoring at fjord scale. The technology addresses a fundamental gap in aquaculture operations: understanding what is happening outside the cages in real-time.

Key takeaways for farm operators:

  • Ocean sound is now formally recognized as an Essential Ocean Variable for biodiversity and ecosystem health
  • Direct evidence shows salmon stress and feeding states are acoustically detectable
  • Cod spawning and presence can be mapped acoustically over large areas
  • PAM provides 24/7 monitoring at a fraction of the cost of conventional methods
  • Initial investment (€40,000-115,000) is small relative to potential loss prevention and production optimization

While quantitative models linking soundscape indices to specific performance metrics are still emerging, the scientific foundation is solid and the operational benefits are clear. Early adopters gain competitive advantage through better risk management, stronger documentation for licensing, and positioning for future biodiversity-linked markets.
