Technical White Paper

Ecosystem Health and Aquaculture Performance

Evidence-based guide for salmon and cod farmers
UnheardHeard • 2025

Executive Summary

Aquaculture executives face a clear message from science: thriving, biodiverse fjord and ocean ecosystems underpin stronger aquaculture performance. Healthy fjord environments – with clean water, balanced nutrients, and intact food webs – correlate with higher fish yields, better growth rates, and lower disease outbreaks in finfish farms.

Conversely, environmental stressors (poor water quality, parasites, harmful algal blooms) have caused major losses in salmon and cod farming, emphasizing that ecosystem management is not just ecology but good business.

This white paper distills high-impact scientific evidence and case studies (Norway, Scotland, Canada) demonstrating how investing in ecosystem health translates to improved aquaculture outcomes. It provides a concise, executive-level overview of why environmental quality equals risk reduction and productivity in salmon and cod operations, and outlines strategic implications for sustainable growth.

1.Scale of Production Losses

1.1 Norway: Current Mortality Data (2023)

Norwegian aquaculture, the world's largest Atlantic salmon producer, recorded unprecedented mortality in 2023. These figures represent the highest losses in the industry's history and demonstrate the direct financial impact of environmental and disease challenges.

Table 1: Norwegian Salmon Mortality Statistics (2023)
Metric Value Context
Total salmon deaths (sea phase) 62.8 million Highest ever recorded
Mortality rate 16.7% Up from 13% in previous years
Rainbow trout mortality 14% (2.5 million) Sea phase production
Estimated revenue loss €2 billion Annual impact

Primary causes identified:

  • Damage from delousing operations
  • Complex gill disease
  • Winter sores
  • Jellyfish damage (new in 2023)
  • Infectious diseases (cardiomyopathy syndrome, heart and skeletal muscle inflammation, pasteurellosis)

Geographic variation is significant: Production Area 3 (PO3) experienced 25% mortality, while PO1, PO11, and PO13 remained below 10%. This variation correlates with local ecosystem conditions and farm density.

Disease represents the single largest constraint on aquaculture productivity worldwide. Studies show that infectious diseases account for approximately half of all production losses, with the impact more severe in developing countries where 90% of aquaculture operations are located.

Viral Disease Prevalence in Norway

Between 2005-2012, Norwegian aquaculture recorded 400-500 viral disease outbreaks annually. The four most frequent viral diseases are:

  • Infectious pancreatic necrosis (IPN) - Declining due to resistant stock
  • Pancreas disease (PD) - 58 cases in 2023 (down from 98 in 2022)
  • Heart and skeletal muscle inflammation (HSMI) - 142 outbreaks in 2012
  • Cardiomyopathy syndrome (CMS) - Growing problem across coastal areas

In 2023, Infectious Salmon Anaemia (ISA) was confirmed at 18 locations, with an additional 5 suspected cases. The concentration of outbreaks in Western Norway highlights the role of farm density and water circulation patterns in disease transmission.

Key Finding: The dominance of viral diseases reflects the lack of effective vaccines for many pathogens. In contrast, bacterial diseases cause only approximately 20 outbreaks annually in Norway, demonstrating that effective vaccination programs can dramatically reduce disease incidence.

2.Harmful Algal Blooms: Catastrophic Risk Events

2.1 Economic Impact of HABs

2.2 HAB Mechanisms and Risk Factors

Harmful algal blooms kill fish through multiple mechanisms:

  • Oxygen depletion: Bloom decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen, causing hypoxic conditions
  • Direct toxicity: Some algal species produce toxins that damage fish gills and tissues
  • Physical gill damage: High biomass clogs gill structures, preventing respiration
  • Light reduction: Dense blooms block light penetration, disrupting ecosystem function

Primary drivers of HAB events:

  • Nutrient enrichment (nitrogen and phosphorus from various sources)
  • Water temperature increases
  • Reduced water circulation and stratification
  • Changes in salinity and pH
Key Finding: While improved monitoring has enhanced HAB detection, the fundamental drivers are ecosystem stressors. The 2016 Chilean event combined nutrient enrichment, warming temperatures, and reduced circulation—all factors influenced by farming intensity and climate change.

3.Climate Change and Marine Heatwaves

3.1 August 2024 Northern Norway Event

In August 2024, northern Norway experienced one of the most intense marine heatwaves on record, providing a clear demonstration of climate change impacts on aquaculture operations.

Event characteristics:

  • Sea surface temperatures exceeded 18°C
  • More than 4°C above long-term average
  • Triggered unprecedented salmon lice outbreak
  • Caused increased fish mortality and substantial economic losses

Compound Effects of Elevated Temperature

The marine heatwave demonstrated how temperature increases create cascading problems:

  1. Accelerated parasite reproduction: Warmer water speeds up salmon lice life cycles
  2. Reduced fish immune function: Temperatures above 17-18°C stress salmon
  3. Decreased feed intake: Prolonged heat exposure reduces appetite
  4. Increased baseline mortality: Heat stress compounds other health challenges
  5. Overwhelmed delousing capacity: Rapid lice reproduction exceeded treatment capacity
Key Finding: Ecosystem stressors don't add linearly—they multiply. A fish stressed by elevated temperature is exponentially more vulnerable to parasites and disease. A warm-water event can transform manageable lice levels into a catastrophic outbreak.

4.2 Ecosystem Services from Healthy Fjord Systems

Healthy fjord ecosystems provide services that directly benefit aquaculture operations:

  • Natural disease suppression: Diverse microbial communities compete with pathogens
  • Parasite control: Wild fish and cleaner species reduce lice populations
  • Water quality buffering: Kelp and other vegetation absorb excess nutrients
  • Oxygen production: Phytoplankton provide dissolved oxygen during daylight
  • Stable temperature regulation: Good circulation prevents thermal stratification

5.Risk Management and Investment Framework

5.1 Cost-Benefit Analysis of Ecosystem Monitoring

Given documented losses from ecosystem degradation, the business case for monitoring investment is straightforward:

Comparative Costs

Advanced monitoring system: €50,000-200,000 initial investment + €20,000-50,000 annual operating costs

Single major HAB event: Potential loss of €500,000-5,000,000+ depending on farm size

Disease outbreak: Can eliminate 15-25% of production value in affected cohort

Return on investment period: Typically 1-3 years if one major event is prevented

5.2 Practical Recommendations for Farm Operators

Immediate Actions (0-6 months)

  • Establish baseline water quality monitoring (temperature, dissolved oxygen, salinity)
  • Join or establish fjord-level monitoring cooperatives with neighboring operations
  • Subscribe to regional HAB early warning systems
  • Review historical mortality data to identify seasonal and environmental patterns

Medium-term Investments (6-18 months)

  • Install continuous monitoring sensors for critical parameters
  • Implement acoustic monitoring for fish behavior and ecosystem changes
  • Develop response protocols for environmental stress events
  • Coordinate with regional authorities on ecosystem health initiatives

Long-term Strategy (18+ months)

  • Integrate ecosystem health data into production planning
  • Pursue sustainability certifications that recognize environmental stewardship
  • Participate in research programs linking ecosystem metrics to performance outcomes
  • Advocate for science-based regional carrying capacity assessments

6.Conclusion

The evidence presented in this white paper demonstrates clear linkages between ecosystem health and aquaculture performance:

  • Disease causes approximately $6 billion in annual global losses, with 50% of production losses attributable to infectious disease
  • Norwegian salmon mortality reached record levels in 2023 (62.8 million fish, 16.7% mortality rate), representing €2 billion in lost revenue
  • Harmful algal blooms cause $8 billion in annual aquaculture losses globally, with individual events (Chile 2016) causing $800 million in damages
  • Climate change is amplifying risks through marine heatwaves and accelerated parasite reproduction

For individual farm operators, ecosystem health monitoring and management represents fundamental risk mitigation. The cost of monitoring systems is a fraction of potential losses from a single major environmental event.

Moreover, operations demonstrating ecosystem stewardship gain market advantages through sustainability certifications and premium pricing, while protecting their long-term license to operate.

The path forward is clear: Invest in ecosystem monitoring, participate in collaborative management at the fjord level, and integrate environmental data into operational decision-making. The technology exists, the business case is proven, and the risks of inaction are well-documented.
