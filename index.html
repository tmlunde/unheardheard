Listening to the Ocean

Aquaculture operators, fisheries, regulators

Hydrophones capture cod and salmon vocalizations, shrimp clicks, and pump sounds to assess fish welfare, feeding behavior, and equipment health.

Why sound: Works in turbid water, day and night; combines animal and mechanical diagnostics in one low-power system.

The Pulse of Kelp Forests

Blue-carbon projects, coastal developers, restoration programs

Underwater sound reveals ecosystem recovery and biodiversity around kelp beds.

Why sound: Sound intensity correlates with life density — a natural metric for carbon-credit verification.

Inside the Barn

Cattle, pig, and poultry producers; animal-welfare certifiers

Ceiling-mounted microphones listen for coughs, squeals, feeding rhythm, and ventilation noise.

Why sound: Detects illness, overcrowding, or equipment faults long before human observation. Healthier herds, lower antibiotics, proof of welfare compliance.

The Living Forest

Forest owners, carbon traders, conservation funds

Acoustic richness and tree bioelectric signals show drought, disease, and biodiversity trends.

Why sound and signal: Early stress detection enables proactive management and credible carbon accounting.

Plants That Speak

Greenhouse growers, vertical farms, smart-garden brands

Electrodes read tiny voltage changes in leaves to automate watering and climate control.

Why signal: Plants reveal stress hours or days before wilting — a silent but measurable language.

Urban Wildlife Resonance

Cities, environmental agencies, real-estate developers

Acoustic nodes map bird, bat, and insect diversity across rooftops and parks.

Why sound: Continuous biodiversity data without cameras or human presence — guiding greener city design.

Planetary Sound Index

Research networks, insurers, policymakers

A global baseline of oceanic, terrestrial, and plant acoustic signatures tracking planetary vitality.

Why sound: Compact, comparable, universal — a living heartbeat of Earth.

Education & Public Awareness

Museums, science centers, schools, environmental NGOs

Interactive installations translate nature's real-time acoustic and electrical signals into light or music.

Why: Builds empathy and understanding — people hear the world they're protecting.
